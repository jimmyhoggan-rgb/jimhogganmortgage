Utah First-Time Home Buyer Guide 2026

Salt Lake City, Wasatch Front & All of Utah — Everything You Need to Know

✍️ Jim Hoggan, NMLS #284263 📅 March 2026 ⏱ 8 min read 🏠 First-Time Buyers

Buying your first home in Utah is one of the most exciting — and most overwhelming — financial decisions you'll ever make. With home prices across the Wasatch Front still elevated and competition strong in many markets, first-time buyers need a clear roadmap to navigate the process confidently.

I've been helping Utah first-time buyers get into homes for 30 years. This guide covers everything you need to know — from your credit score to closing day.

Step 1: Know Your Credit Score

Your credit score is the single most important number in your mortgage application. It determines whether you qualify and what interest rate you'll receive.

Here's what different score ranges mean for Utah buyers:

  • 740+ — Best rates available on all loan programs
  • 700–739 — Strong qualification, very competitive rates
  • 660–699 — Good qualification for FHA and conventional
  • 620–659 — FHA eligible; limited conventional options
  • Below 620 — FHA possible with compensating factors; consult a loan officer

Before applying, pull your free credit reports at AnnualCreditReport.com and dispute any errors. Even small errors can cost you a better rate.

Step 2: Understand Your Down Payment Options

One of the biggest myths about buying a home is that you need 20% down. Most Utah first-time buyers put down far less — and there are programs specifically designed to help.

FHA Loans — 3.5% Down

FHA loans are backed by the Federal Housing Administration and are the most popular choice for Utah first-time buyers. With a credit score of 580 or higher, you can put down as little as 3.5%.

Conventional Loans — 3% Down

With strong credit (typically 620+), conventional loans allow down payments as low as 3%. Once you reach 20% equity, you can remove private mortgage insurance (PMI).

VA Loans — 0% Down

If you've served in the military or are an active-duty service member, you may qualify for a VA loan with zero down payment and no private mortgage insurance. This is one of the most valuable home financing benefits available to eligible Utah veterans.

Utah Housing Down Payment Assistance

Utah Housing Corporation offers down payment assistance programs for qualifying first-time buyers through participating lenders. Income and purchase price limits apply.

Important: Down payment assistance availability, income limits, and purchase price limits change periodically. Contact a participating lender for current program details and eligibility requirements.

Step 3: Get Pre-Approved Before You Shop

A pre-approval letter is your ticket to be taken seriously in Utah's competitive market. Sellers in Salt Lake City, Utah County, and the Wasatch Front regularly receive multiple offers — and offers without pre-approval letters are often ignored.

To get pre-approved, your loan officer will review:

  • Your last 2 years of W-2s and tax returns
  • Your last 30 days of pay stubs
  • Your last 2–3 months of bank statements
  • Your credit report and score
  • Any other income sources (rental income, alimony, etc.)

Pre-approval is not the same as pre-qualification. A pre-approval involves a full credit pull and income verification — it carries real weight with sellers and their agents.

Step 4: Understand the True Cost of Homeownership

Your mortgage payment is just one piece of the picture. Before you fall in love with a home, make sure you understand all the costs:

  • Principal and interest — your actual loan payment
  • Property taxes — varies significantly by county in Utah
  • Homeowners insurance — typically $800–$1,500/year in Utah
  • HOA fees — if the property is in a homeowners association
  • Private mortgage insurance (PMI) — if you put less than 20% down on a conventional loan
  • Maintenance and repairs — budget 1–2% of home value annually

Step 5: The Purchase Process Step by Step

Here's how the home buying process works in Utah from start to finish:

  1. Get pre-approved with a loan officer
  2. Work with a real estate agent to find homes in your budget
  3. Make an offer — your agent will guide you on terms and price
  4. Go under contract — seller accepts your offer
  5. Home inspection — hire an independent inspector
  6. Appraisal — your lender orders an appraisal to confirm value
  7. Loan processing and underwriting — your lender finalizes approval
  8. Clear to close — all conditions satisfied
  9. Final walkthrough — confirm the home is in agreed condition
  10. Closing day — sign documents, fund the loan, get your keys

Common First-Time Buyer Mistakes to Avoid

  • Opening new credit accounts or making large purchases during the loan process
  • Changing jobs before or during the application process
  • Making large deposits into your bank account without documentation
  • Skipping the home inspection to save money
  • Buying at the absolute top of your pre-approved amount — leave yourself a cushion
