How Much Home Can You Afford in Salt Lake City?

A Utah Mortgage Guide to Budgeting, DTI, and Buying Power in Today's Market

✍️ Jim Hoggan, NMLS #284263 📅 March 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 🏠 Buyers & Budgeting

Salt Lake City's housing market has changed dramatically over the past several years. Prices that seemed high in 2019 now look like bargains compared to today's market. With median home prices in Salt Lake County ranging from the high $400s to well over $600,000 in desirable neighborhoods, figuring out what you can truly afford is more important than ever.

Here's how mortgage lenders actually calculate your buying power — and what you can do to maximize it.

The Key Number: Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI)

When a lender evaluates your application, the most important calculation is your debt-to-income ratio — your total monthly debt payments divided by your gross monthly income.

There are two DTI numbers lenders look at:

  • Front-end DTI — your proposed housing payment (principal, interest, taxes, insurance, HOA) divided by your gross monthly income. Most lenders want this below 28–31%.
  • Back-end DTI — all monthly debt payments including your housing payment, car payments, student loans, credit cards, and other obligations. Most conventional loans require this below 43–45%.

Example: If you earn $7,500/month gross and have $500/month in existing debt payments, lenders may allow a maximum housing payment of around $2,875 (back-end DTI of 45%), minus the $500 in existing debt — leaving roughly $2,375 for your mortgage payment.

Salt Lake City Home Affordability by Income

Here are general estimates of purchase price ranges at different income levels in today's market. These are estimates only — your actual qualification depends on your specific financial picture.

  • $60,000/year income — approximately $250,000–$300,000 purchase price
  • $80,000/year income — approximately $330,000–$390,000 purchase price
  • $100,000/year income — approximately $410,000–$490,000 purchase price
  • $120,000/year income — approximately $490,000–$590,000 purchase price
  • $150,000/year income — approximately $620,000–$740,000 purchase price

Note: These ranges assume approximately 10% down and good credit. They shift significantly based on your down payment, existing debt, credit score, and current interest rate. A loan officer can give you a precise number based on your actual situation.

The Impact of Interest Rates on Affordability

Interest rates have a dramatic effect on your monthly payment and how much home you can afford. Here's how a $400,000 loan payment changes at different rates:

  • 5.5% rate — approximately $2,271/month (principal and interest)
  • 6.5% rate — approximately $2,528/month
  • 7.0% rate — approximately $2,661/month
  • 7.5% rate — approximately $2,797/month

That's a difference of over $500/month between a 5.5% and 7.5% rate on the same loan amount — which is why finding the right loan program and rate for your situation matters so much.

Down Payment: How It Affects Your Buying Power

A larger down payment reduces your loan amount, eliminates or reduces mortgage insurance costs, and can get you a better interest rate. Here's how different down payments affect a $450,000 purchase:

  • 3.5% down ($15,750) — FHA loan, includes MIP of approximately $238/month
  • 5% down ($22,500) — Conventional with PMI of approximately $150–200/month
  • 10% down ($45,000) — Conventional, lower PMI around $100–150/month
  • 20% down ($90,000) — Conventional, no PMI

Salt Lake City Neighborhoods and What They Cost

Salt Lake City is not one market — it's many. Here's a general range of what buyers are seeing in different areas:

  • The Avenues — $600,000–$900,000+ for single family homes
  • Sugar House — $500,000–$750,000
  • Millcreek / Holladay — $500,000–$800,000
  • South Jordan / Herriman — $450,000–$650,000
  • West Valley / Taylorsville — $350,000–$500,000
  • Murray — $400,000–$600,000

Note: Prices shift constantly — these are general ranges. Your real estate agent will have the most current data for specific neighborhoods.

How to Increase Your Buying Power

If you're not where you need to be yet, here are the most effective ways to increase your purchasing power:

  • Pay down existing debt — reducing your monthly debt payments increases how much mortgage you qualify for
  • Improve your credit score — even moving from 680 to 720 can meaningfully improve your rate
  • Save a larger down payment — reduces your loan amount and eliminates PMI sooner
  • Add a co-borrower — a spouse or partner's income can significantly increase your qualification
  • Explore different loan programs — FHA, VA, USDA, and down payment assistance programs each have different qualification criteria
